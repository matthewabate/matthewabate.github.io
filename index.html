What I Do

Identifying the effort worth making.

Before any organization starts designing or developing anything, someone needs to do the harder work of understanding the problem. That means getting out into the world, seeing how people really behave, sifting through the existing mess, and asking better questions than "what do you want?"

In order to find our way out of the fog of ambiguity, we need to choose a direction.

My career started in digital. I've been doing this since before websites could have images. But the work that's meant the most to me, and where I've done my best work, has almost always been useful in the real world. Stadiums. Hospitals. Automotive showrooms. Financial service centers. Environments where a screen or someone's data might be part of the answer, but the interactions between people and how a product or service affects our physical space matter much more. Misjudging what people need from what we make will sink whatever the digital piece does right.

Those are the kinds of problems I'm looking for: not pure digital, not technology trying to find a problem, but the whole physical-digital experience and how it either comes together for the people moving through it.

The output isn't a design. It's a clear, evidence-backed strategy with the research to defend it and a roadmap someone can act on.

How I got here

I started out designing things: websites, human-machine interfaces, apps. It taught me how to make complex ideas legible and how to defend aesthetic decisions with logic.

I moved into digital products and learned what it meant to design for life-changing behavioral outcomes, not just appearance. Research, iteration, and systems thinking became the infrastructure behind my decisions.

Now I work upstream, before the wireframes, before the brief, before anyone has agreed on what the problem actually is. That's where I've done my most rewarding work.

My professional experience spans Healthcare, Sports & Entertainment, Automotive, Financial Services, Retail, Media, Technology, Non-Profits, and Education.
How I Work

A few frameworks I keep in my quiver.

I believe strategy needs a variety of methods if it's going to have an impact. I reach for these three tools in almost every engagement to help cut through ambiguity and find something that will provide real value for the people we are designing for.

Desirability · Feasibility · Viability

Most initiatives fail because they are dominated by one dimension and neglect the others. Desirability asks whether people will benefit from what we make (and why). Feasibility asks whether the organization can build and sustain it (and is willing to do what it takes). Viability asks whether it's differentiated enough to be worth doing ($$$). Applying all three is how you avoid expensive mistakes downstream, but starting from Desirability is how you make something special and lasting.

Jobs to Be Done &
Journey Mapping

People don't want products. They want to experience something that matters to them. JTBD is a framework for understanding underlying motivations (as opposed to what people say they want) and what they're actually trying to accomplish. JTBD enhances Journey Maps by layering insights over each touchpoint in the journey. It cuts through feature requests and stakeholder opinions to find real and addressable problems, which are the only ones worth solving.

Design Thinking &
Service Design

Design Thinking gives us a human-centered process for moving from ambiguous problem to testable direction. Service Design expands that thinking beyond single touchpoints to map the full experience across channels, staff, physical environments, and operational systems. Together they help uncover what's actually happening and what a better version of it could look like.

What's my AI experience?

AI as the Engine

In 2016 I got my first mandate to use AI to replace people. Because we were making digital therapeutics, I flipped this to apply AI where it genuinely improved the experience, and kept humans in the loop where it didn't. That meant automating clear-cut decisions and routing nuanced issues to experts. Today I continue to ask where AI makes things genuinely better and helps the person who used to do everything.

Rapid Prototyping

I build working prototypes with AI as my engineering team. Mockups are critical at certain phases of thinking, but functional prototypes are my favorite way to get people to talk about what they need from the experience. This changes how decisions get made and how much confidence organizations have before they invest.

Research & Synthesis

Finding patterns across interview transcripts, scaffolding secondary research, synthesizing large datasets requires tons of human hours. I use AI to move faster through the parts that don't require judgment, and collaborate with my team to find insights and make decisions. The output is better synthesis, not fewer people.

Leading through collaboration

What it looks like to work with me.

Every problem seems unique, and often feels overwhelming. I approach every engagement with an attitude that anything can be solved, and I strive to make everyone on the team valuable.

1

Discovery

My first job is to examine the decisions, assumptions, and hypotheses already baked into the project when it kicks off. We need to understand what we know, but what we don't know can be even more important to make sure we're solving the right problems.

2

Research

Get out into the world. Survey a lot of people, observe them in the wild, interview people living with the problem. Organizations are often surprised what people are really trying to accomplish.

3

Synthesis

Turn over everything we learned to notice patterns and find opportunities to create value for people. Be clear enough to answer questions on the fly and hold up under scrutiny. The goal is a shared understanding of what's true that earns its place in ongoing decisions and permeates everything the organization does.

4

Strategy

To figure out what insights mean for the business, I come looking for the right answer, not attached to one I brought with me. Which opportunities are real? Which are worth pursuing? What should we not do? This is where desirability, feasibility, and viability all have to answer for themselves independently.

5

Roadmap

At this point leadership should be excited by what we've put together, and some of it may feel obvious. I work with them to sequence priorities and define what needs validation before committing. The goal is a plan the organization can actually execute and feels achievable rather than overwhelming. When the work is done, you should know exactly where you're going and feel confident you will get there.

My experience

Solving problems since 1998.

I've worked in a broad range of industries: Healthcare, Sports & Entertainment, Automotive, Financial Services, Retail, Media, Technology, Non-Profits, and Education. I've worked at agencies, as a consultant, and in-house at early-stage startups and global enterprises. All of them involved real complexity and a narrow margin for getting things wrong.

I went to school for magazine design but quickly switched to digital. I enjoyed making interfaces and user experiences, but at some point I realized figuring out what should be made was more fulfilling than deciding what it should look like. My work now lives upstream of crafting the final product. I am more energized when I am understanding the problem, defining the opportunity, helping organizations make confident decisions about where to put their effort.

I'm drawn to problems with physical or environmental dimension. The way a service unfolds across a space. How an organization delivers an experience that isn't purely screen based. The friction between how something was designed to work and how people actually experience it. That's the interesting stuff, and I've spent a long time getting good at it.
